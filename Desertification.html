Desertification

is a type of land degradation in drylands in which biological productivity is lost due to natural processes or induced by human activities whereby fertile areas become arid.[1][2] The concept of 'desertification' typically evokes mental imagery of the progressive expansion of arid regions, encroaching sand dunes, and an overall transformation of once fertile landscapes into barren and inhospitable terrain.[3] It is the spread of arid areas caused by a variety of factors, such as overexploitation of soil as a result of human activity and the effects of climate change.Desertification stands as a pressing environmental issue, with the potential to impact approximately 35% of Earth's land surface and affect around 32% of the global human population.[7] This phenomenon pertains to the deterioration of land in arid, semi-arid, and dry sub-humid regions, encompassing the degradation of vegetation cover, soil quality, and nutrient depletion.

Types of Desertification


Aridification

This is the type of desertification that occurs in arid regions, where the rainfall is insufficient to sustain vegetation and soil fertility


Salanization

This is the type of desertification that occurs when the soil becomes too salty for plants to grow


Soil Erosion

This is the type of desertification that occurs when the topsoil is removed by wind or water, leaving behind a degraded layer of subsoil or bedrock.


Soil Nutrient depletion

This is the type of desertification that occurs when the soil loses its essential nutrients for plant growth, such as nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, or organic matter.

Effects of Desertification

Loss of biodiversity

Desertification can reduce the diversity and abundance of plants and animals that depend on the drylands for their habitat and food. This can affect the ecosystem services and functions that they provide, such as pollination, seed dispersal, soil formation, or carbon sequestration

Food insecurity

Desertification can reduce the agricultural productivity and food availability in the drylands, leading to hunger, malnutrition, and poverty. This can also increase the dependence on food imports, which can be costly and unreliable

Water scarcity

Desertification can reduce the water resources and quality in the drylands, affecting the domestic, agricultural, and industrial uses of water. This can also increase the risk of water conflicts, diseases, and sanitation problems

Migration and conflict

Desertification can force people to migrate from the drylands to other areas, in search of better living conditions and opportunities. This can create social, economic, and political challenges, such as overcrowding, unemployment, discrimination, or violence

Controlling Methods


Using seed drills instead of tilling to prevent soil erosion and conserve crop residue. This can help maintain soil fertility and moisture


Trying non-traditional crops that are more adapted to dry conditions and can improve soil quality. Some examples are barley, wheat, sunflowers, chickpeas, millet, and sorghum


Reducing the number of grazing animals and increasing the area of cropland. This can help prevent overgrazing and restore vegetation cover


Improving water management by using techniques such as earth dams, magic stones, and drip irrigation. These can help store and distribute water more efficiently and reduce water loss


Causes

Overgrazing

This is when animals eat too much of the vegetation in an area, leaving the soil exposed and vulnerable to erosion and loss of nutrients. Overgrazing can be caused by increasing the number of livestock, reducing the grazing area, or extending the grazing season

Deforestation

This is when trees and other plants are cut down or burned, either for timber, fuel, or land clearing. Deforestation can reduce the water cycle, increase soil erosion, lower soil fertility, and release greenhouse gase

Unsustainable agriculture techniques

This is when farming practices degrade the soil quality and productivity, such as monocropping, excessive use of fertilizers or pesticides, or tilling the soil. These techniques can reduce the organic matter, nutrients, and moisture in the soil, making it more prone to desertification

Climate change

This is when the global temperature and precipitation patterns change due to human activities, such as burning fossil fuels, deforestation, or agriculture. Climate change can increase the frequency and intensity of droughts, heat waves, floods, and storms, which can affect the vegetation and soil conditions in drylands

Overpopulation and overexploitation of natural resources

This is when the human population grows beyond the carrying capacity of the land, leading to increased demand and consumption of water, food, energy, and materials. This can result in overpumping of groundwater, overfishing, mining, or pollution, which can deplete and degrade the natural resources and ecosystems
